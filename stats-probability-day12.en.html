Bayesian Inference

Probability as belief, updated by evidence
Probability as Belief · Priors and Updating
CORE INSIGHT

Probability is not an objective property of the world — it is a degree of belief. New evidence shouldn't erase prior belief but reweight it via "prior × likelihood = posterior." Ignoring base rates is the single most systematic error in human intuitive probability — among doctors, judges, and investors alike.

ORIGIN & MECHANISM

Thomas Bayes (1701-1761), a Presbyterian minister, was published posthumously in 1763 by Richard Price ("An Essay towards solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances"); Laplace independently rediscovered and extended it (1774) to celestial measurement. For much of the 20th century, frequentists (Fisher, Neyman, Pearson) dismissed Bayesianism as "subjective." Only E. T. Jaynes' Probability Theory: The Logic of Science — recasting Bayesian probability as extended logic — plus the 1990s MCMC computational revolution put it back in the mainstream. The formula P(H|D) = P(D|H)·P(H)/P(D) is trivial; the hard part is realizing every probability is conditional on what you know — there is no information-free probability.

COUNTERINTUITIVE EXAMPLE

Casscells, Schoenberger & Grayboys (NEJM, 1978) posed the above puzzle to faculty and students at Harvard Medical School. Only 18% got an answer near correct; the average estimate was 95%. Physicians' base-rate neglect was no better than the public's. The Sally Clark case (UK 1999): both prosecution and defense treated "P(two SIDS deaths) = P(one)² = 1 in 73,000,000" as evidence of guilt. The Royal Statistical Society publicly protested in 2002 — flawed independence assumptions plus base-rate error jointly produced a wrongful conviction.

CROSS-DISCIPLINARY TRANSFER

In machine learning, Bayesian networks, variational inference, conjugate priors, and Bayesian Deep Learning anchor uncertainty modeling; in cognitive science, Friston's "free-energy principle" and Clark's "predictive brain" argue the brain is a hierarchical Bayesian inference machine; in evidence-based medicine, Bayesian diagnostic reasoning is now standard; in AI alignment, RLHF reward modeling and Constitutional AI iterative correction are Bayesian updates in disguise; in finance, investors "find religion" after black swan events — really just a violent prior update.

BIGCAT APPLICATION + REFLECTION

"That person always procrastinates" is usually base-rate error — every project slips (base rate ~80%); their personal slip rate barely updates anything. Conversely, the optimistic "this launch will be huge" routinely ignores the prior that 90% of industry launches barely ripple. Train the reflex of asking "before I saw this evidence, what was my prior on H? Is the likelihood ratio P(D|H)/P(D|¬H) really 20x or just 2x?" — it kills most hasty conclusions. Same with kids: three wrong problems shouldn't update you to "she's bad at math" — three problems against a semester of hundreds can't move any prior worth holding.

▸ Reflection: Take your most recent "I knew it" judgment and write it as P(H|D). What was your prior? Does the likelihood ratio really support that posterior jump?

Monte Carlo Methods

Trading computation for analytical insight
Random Sampling · Compute Over Cleverness
CORE INSIGHT

When a problem can't be solved analytically but can be simulated, repeated random sampling converges to arbitrary precision — turning "thinking" into "number of trials." This dissolves the old equation "complex = unsolvable": many problems once treated as deep math are today just compute problems.

ORIGIN & MECHANISM

In 1946, Stanislaw Ulam — bedridden, playing solitaire — tried to compute his winning probability, found analysis hopeless but simulation trivial. Back at Los Alamos he and von Neumann ran the first systematic application on ENIAC, simulating neutron diffusion for the hydrogen bomb. Nicholas Metropolis named it (after the Monaco casino). The theoretical backbone is the law of large numbers: any integral, expectation, or path sum becomes "sample then average." The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm (1953), Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, sequential Monte Carlo, and MCTS all descend from this idea — using randomness to precisely approximate determinism.

COUNTERINTUITIVE EXAMPLE

Estimating π: scatter N uniform points in a 1×1 square; the fraction landing in the inscribed quarter-circle, times 4, gives π. 10⁴ points → ~3.14; 10⁶ → ~3.1416. Not a line of geometry — just dice. The same idea now powers exotic derivative pricing beyond Black-Scholes, Bayesian posterior sampling (modern Bayesian inference is basically MCMC), AlphaGo's MCTS (thousands of simulated games per move), and every large language model's token-by-token sampling — your chat with an LLM is essentially shallow Monte Carlo.

CROSS-DISCIPLINARY TRANSFER

In physics, quantum Monte Carlo for many-body problems; in finance, VaR, CVaR, derivative pricing; in statistics, Gibbs sampling, NUTS, and variational inference made modern Bayesian methods tractable; in game AI, MCTS is the heart of AlphaGo/MuZero; in climate science, ensemble forecasts perturb initial conditions to sample the future distribution; in drug discovery, molecular conformational sampling; in robotics, particle filters for localization and tracking.

BIGCAT APPLICATION + REFLECTION

For complex decisions (change jobs? will the project ship?), the common error is trying to "calculate" rather than to "sample." Sketching three or four scenarios and concluding "60% it'll work" is far weaker than simulating 20 concrete execution paths (including extreme edges) and noting which failure modes recur. Taleb calls it "path thinking" — the outcome distribution matters far more than the expected value. Same for parenting: don't make "the perfect plan" — first simulate ten variants of "Wednesday night the kid's sick and you're still on a call at 8 pm." That's where the P95 stress lives. Treating decisions as samplable random processes is one of the highest-leverage transfers from engineering into life.

▸ Reflection: For your most recent big decision, can you "sample" 10 concrete paths rather than only computing expectations? Which path most unsettles you? Is it actually in your contingency plan?

Survivorship Bias

The data you can see is selected — the failures are silent
Selection Bias · Silent Data
CORE INSIGHT

When you only observe "survivors," your conclusions are almost certainly wrong. Failures are silent; what looks like "success factors" may be noise specific to survivors, not real causation. The deadliest bias is not in the data itself but in the data-generating process — what you see has been filtered by "what samples could reach you."

ORIGIN & MECHANISM

The canonical case is Abraham Wald at the WWII Statistical Research Group. The US Air Force wanted to armor returning bombers where bullet holes clustered — wings and tail. Wald reversed it: armor where the holes are absent (engines, cockpit) — because planes hit there never returned. An extreme form of selection bias: samples filtered on survival. Generalized: any data conditional on survival overestimates upside and underestimates risk. Berkson's paradox, publication bias, and liquidation bias are all incarnations of the same phenomenon in different domains.

COUNTERINTUITIVE EXAMPLE

1990s "success literature" uniformly found Fortune-500 CEOs share traits like "wake early, read, persevere" — but the same traits are likely just as common in bankrupt CEOs. Phil Rosenzweig's The Halo Effect (2007) showed Jim Collins' "great companies" in Good to Great mostly performed mediocrely or died within five years of publication — under survivorship sampling, post-hoc attribution is pure narrative. Hedge-fund median returns look glossy because failing funds liquidate out of the database (Brown, Goetzmann, Ibbotson & Ross 1992 showed "liquidation bias" inflates reported returns by ~3% annually).

CROSS-DISCIPLINARY TRANSFER

In history, "ancient buildings were all sturdy" — the fragile ones crumbled long ago; in clinical medicine, only positive results get published (publication bias), driving the literature's optimism and fueling the replication crisis; in machine learning, training data is itself a filtered sample ("offline metrics great, production bad" often originates here); in migration studies, "immigrants are harder-working" ignores that "lazy people don't emigrate" is the selection; in education, "Harvard graduates earn more" ignores that "being admitted" is the strong signal; in AI benchmarks, tuning on public tasks systematically overestimates "in-the-wild" performance.

BIGCAT APPLICATION + REFLECTION

The trap technologists fall into most is only studying successful open-source projects, startups, and AI products, treating their designs as scripture. A debiasing drill: for every success story you read, force yourself to find three failed cases that used the same strategy. If you can't find them, either the failures had no voice, or the "strategy" isn't identifiable — in both cases, distrust the attribution. Same for parenting: before copying "Tsinghua mom shares her kid-raising secrets," ask "where are the moms who used the same method but whose kids didn't get in?" Their silence doesn't mean they don't exist.

▸ Reflection: For a "success path" you're currently emulating, can you list three failed cases using the same method? If not, are you following a pattern or following noise?

Simpson's Paradox

Aggregate flips the sign — confounders decide direction
Confounding Variables · Gateway to Causal Inference
CORE INSIGHT

A may be better than B in aggregate, yet B may be better than A in every subgroup — summation can flip the sign. This means the direction of a statistical correlation depends on whether you've controlled for the right confounder. It's not that "data lies"; it's that data without a causal model has no direction — this is the watershed from descriptive statistics into causal inference.

ORIGIN & MECHANISM

Edward Simpson formalized it in 1951 (Pearson noted a precursor in 1899). The most famous case: 1973 UC Berkeley graduate admissions — men admitted at 44%, women at 35%; superficially, gender discrimination. Bickel, Hammel & O'Connell (Science, 1975), going department by department, found nearly every department admitted women at rates equal to or higher than men. The confounder was "choice of department" — women disproportionately applied to humanities, where admit rates were lower for everyone. Pearl's do-calculus gives the rigorous criterion: whether to condition on a variable Z depends on whether Z is a confounder, mediator, or collider — blind stratification and blind aggregation both manufacture paradoxes.

COUNTERINTUITIVE EXAMPLE

Charig et al. (BMJ, 1986) on kidney-stone treatments A vs B: overall A succeeds 78%, B 83% — B looks better. But stratifying by stone size: small stones A 93% > B 87%; large stones A 73% > B 69% — A wins regardless of size. The reason: physicians assigned the harder, larger stones disproportionately to A. The "which treatment is better" answer depends on whether you're asking about the treatment's causal effect or about its marginal performance under the physicians' assignment policy — two different causal questions on identical data.

CROSS-DISCIPLINARY TRANSFER

In causal inference, Pearl's do-calculus and causal graphs (2011 ACM Turing Award) exist precisely to disentangle this; in A/B testing, Simpson reversal is the analyst's most common trap (user-group differences hide the real effect); in ML fairness, "the algorithm is more accurate in every subgroup but less overall" (or vice versa); in epidemiology, vaccine effectiveness must be stratified by age; in education, "this school's admission rate is high" may come entirely from incoming student quality; in benchmarks, model A winning every dataset yet losing the aggregate score.

BIGCAT APPLICATION + REFLECTION

The biggest trap in data-driven decisions isn't "missing data" — it's aggregated data. Team productivity is up 10%, but it may all come from one new high-output member while everyone else declined; DAU is up 20%, but new users may be less engaged than churned users, so apparent growth is actually dilution. For any key metric, immediately ask "after which stratification does this still hold?" — this is the gulf between engineers and vanity metrics. Same in parenting: kid finished homework faster this month — real progress or easier problem set? Without proper stratification, every "improvement" judgment may be reversed.

▸ Reflection: For a recent "clearly improving / worsening" metric in your team, product, or family, does it hold after stratifying three different ways? Which stratification flips the conclusion?
